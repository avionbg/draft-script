### Chapter Text

The morning watch handed off at first light with nothing to report, which Elara had learned meant something was wrong. Smooth crossings announced themselves with small friction: a rope needing re-coiling, a compass bearing three degrees off, someone's boots left on the wrong side of the hatch. Silence was the sea's way of concentrating your attention.

She checked the heading herself. Correct to the mark, which was almost worse.

Denn was already in the engine bay when she descended. He did not look up.

"How long has it been running like this?" she asked.

"Since before Calloway Point." He wiped his hands on a cloth that would not get any cleaner. "I logged it. Nobody read the log."

Elara crouched beside the secondary housing and felt the heat through her palm without touching the casing. Too much. The kind of heat that meant the problem was not going to solve itself overnight.

"We can reduce speed by a third and buy another four days," she said.

"Or we make port in six and have a real mechanic look at it."

"Six days assumes the wind holds."

"Six days assumes a lot of things." He finally looked at her. "You know what's in that hold."

She didn't answer.

"Someone does," he said. "And whoever it is decided the schedule matters more than the drive."

She straightened up and listened to the sound the engine was making — a low, even thrum that had no right to sound so calm given what the temperature gauge was reading. It reminded her of people who smiled while they were furious. The most dangerous kind of composure.

"I'll talk to Voss," she said.

Denn made a short sound that was not quite a laugh. "Let me know how that goes."

The corridor to the captain's quarters was three minutes at a walk and felt longer. She knocked twice and waited. The door opened almost immediately, which meant Voss had been awake and listening.

"I know about the drive," he said, before she could speak. "Sit down."

There was one chair. She sat.

"We will not be reducing speed," he said. "We will not be detouring. We will arrive on schedule."

"The secondary could fail before we make port."

"It won't."

"You can't know that."

He looked at her with the particular patience of someone who has already made a decision and is waiting for the room to catch up. "I know more about this crossing than you do, Elara. That's not an insult. It's a fact. When we arrive, everything will be explained. Until then, I need you to trust that I have reasons."

She thought about the sealed hold. About Denn's face. About the heat coming off the housing.

"And if I don't?" she said.

The captain looked out the small porthole at the grey water moving past. "Then we'll have a harder six days than we need to."

She left without another word. In the corridor, she stopped and pressed one hand flat against the bulkhead. Cold iron. Solid. Still.

The sea outside gave nothing away.
